Analytics Reports

Our analytics report provides information on the rice production, importation, prices and challenges.

Production: The rice production from 2012 to 2021 is fairly fluid with a total of 187.63 metric tons of rice that is produced in 10 years.

Imports and exports: The rice exports from 2012 to 2021 was not great in number with 2013 is the highest revenue of rice export with 5,358,550 USD in that year but it goes down so much in 2015 to 2021. In 2020 Philippines exported rice to United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, Bangladesh, Qatar and United States. In rice imports from 2012 to 2021 is so high that 2021 has 1,196,910,000 USD. In 2020 alone we imported from Vietnam, Burma, China, Thailand and India.

Consumption and demand: The consumption of rice from 2012 to 2021 was good although rice is high in demand the production of rice per metric tons is more than the consumption per metric tons of the people here in the Philippines.

Prices and market trends: The government buys palay in average farmgate price for 17.68 Php per kilogram. This was the price of palay in last quarter of 2022. The huge factor for influencing the price movement of rice was the supply and demand of it, Income and population in the Philippines.

Government policies and Initiatives: The main source of assistance for Filipino manufacturers is market price support (MPS), which reflects the country's trade restrictions (primarily tariffs and Tariff Rate Quotas - TRQs). The primary beneficiaries of price support schemes are rice producers. Together with rice, significant amounts of support are given to sugarcane, pig meat, and poultry, especially through high import taxes. Due to the high MPS, main consumers, such as the food processing sector, are implicitly taxed. The ratio of general service expenditures (GSSE) to agricultural value added has risen recently. The majority of general service spending goes into extension programs and the improvement of infrastructure, particularly irrigation systems. In 2017–19, the total cost of assistance provided to the Philippine agriculture sector amounted to 2.9% of GDP, ranking it as one of the highest costs among all countries studied.

Challenges: Many Filipino farmers are simply unable to take their production further due to the high expense of many critical inputs. Compounding this is the low palay prices that could be expected after harvesting, which keeps many farmers in a perpetual state of struggle and often in serious debt. Climate change is causing seasonal typhoons to happen more frequently and with more ferocity while leading to dry seasons with unheard-of high temperatures. Both of these situations are damaging enough for most farms’ productivity. However, the negative effects of climate change do not stop there. The market for rice has changed much over the past generation. Not only are cheaper imports from Vietnam and other Asian countries pricing Filipino farmers out, but domestic rice consumption has also been dropping slowly over the past few decades. Fewer and fewer farmers want their children to have the life that they do. As a result, most of them do everything they can to ensure their children get better-paying jobs, often in the cities. As a result, the average age of Filipino farmers is 53, as few younger people are willing to take up farming. This means that productivity per farmer can be expected to fall, especially for such physically demanding crops as rice.

Trends

Here are some of the most common type of rice in the country.

1. Long-grain rice - This is the most commonly consumed type of rice in the Philippines, and is typically used for everyday meals.

2. Medium-grain rice - This type of rice is stickier and has a softer texture than long-grain rice, and is often used for making sushi or other dishes that require sticky rice.

3. Short-grain rice - This type of rice is very sticky and has a very soft texture, and is often used for making desserts or other sweet dishes.

4. Glutinous rice - This type of rice is very sticky and is often used for making traditional Filipino desserts like suman and bibingka.

5. Fragrant rice - This type of rice has a distinct aroma and is often used for special occasions or for making dishes that require a more fragrant rice.

6. Colored rice - This includes red, black, and brown rice varieties that have a higher nutritional value than white rice, and are becoming increasingly popular among health-conscious consumers.

This are the stop selling commercial rice varieties in the Philippines according to the industry reports and market research.

1. Jasmine Rice - This is a fragrant long-grain rice variety that is popular in the Philippines, especially in high-end restaurants and hotels. It is known for its soft texture and fragrant aroma.

2. Dinorado Rice - This is a high-quality aromatic rice that is grown in the Cordillera region of the Philippines. It is popular among consumers who are willing to pay a premium for its quality and taste.

3. Sinandomeng Rice - This is a popular variety of rice that is grown in Central Luzon. It is known for its soft texture and sweet flavor, and is often used in traditional Filipino dishes such as adobo and sinigang.

4. IR64 Rice - This is a high-yielding variety of rice that is grown in many parts of the Philippines. It is known for its resistance to pests and diseases, as well as its excellent cooking qualities. It is often used in commercial rice production.

5. Angelica Rice - This is a popular variety of rice that is grown in the Visayas region of the Philippines. It is known for its soft texture and good cooking quality, and is often used in traditional Filipino dishes such as fried rice and arroz caldo.

These are the top 5 commercial rice varieties in the Philippines in the last ten years (2011-2020). This data is from Philippine Rice Information System (PRISM) of the department of agriculture.

1. NSIC Rc222 - This high-yielding variety developed by the Philippine Rice Research Institute (PhilRice) has consistently been the most planted rice variety in the Philippines in the last ten years, accounting for an average of 13.8% of total rice area.

Rice Import

According to the data from the Philippne Statics Authority (PSA), these are the most imported rice in the Philippines

1. Indica rice - This is a variety of long-grain rice that is commonly grown in countries such as Thailand, Vietnam, and India. In 2020, it was the most imported rice variety in the Philippines, accounting for 73.14% of total rice imports.

2. Japonica rice - This is a variety of short-grain rice that is commonly grown in Japan, Korea, and China. In 2020, it was the second most imported rice variety in the Philippines, accounting for 15.05% of total rice imports.

3. Aromatic rice - This includes rice varieties such as Basmati and Jasmine, which are known for their distinct aroma and flavor. In 2020, aromatic rice accounted for 11.81% of total rice imports in the Philippines.

Other rice varieties that are imported in smaller quantities in the Philippines include glutinous rice, specialty rice, and parboiled rice.

Rice Export

The Philippines is not a major exporter of rice, as it typically imports more rice than it produces. In fact, the country has been a net rice importer since the 1990s. While there may be some limited export of rice varieties, it is not a significant contributor to the country's economy. The country's rice production is mainly used to meet domestic demand. Therefore, there are no significant exports of rice from the Philippines, and the country's rice imports generally exceed its exports.
